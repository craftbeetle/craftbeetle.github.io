FILMS

My thoughts on the films I have watched. This list only goes back a few years but in that time I have watched a lot of movies. I don't have a lot to say about every movie, sometimes it's just something I watched a few years back.

Children Of Shatila

Another documentary film by the maker of Frontiers of Dreams and Fears, Children of Shatila follows survivors of the Zionist massacre of Sabra and Shatila and their descendants, specifically two children named Issa and Farah.

We follow the human toll of the continues evils of the Zionist project and are reminded once more that this did not start with Operation Al-Aqsa Flood. The massacre as Sabra and Shatila were conducted on 16–18 September 1982, and they are not unique in colonial history.

All colonized peoples have a Sabra and Shatila, and thus we are bound together to resist these evils and end imperialism once and for all.

Resistance - Why?

To understand the path forward in the mission of Palestinian liberation and global anti-colonialism, we must necessary study those who came before us, so that we can effectively pick up where they left off.

This film is a direct communication from the thinkers who came before us, most famously Ghassan Kanafani, as they outline for us their current conditions and they goals. Watching this film gave me a sense of continuity and duty. People came before me and lived, struggled, gave a lot and in many cases gave everything for the liberation movement and our duty is to continue their work.

Office Space

I laughed a few times. I found some moments funny, the movie as a whole was entertaining. It stayed safely at the very surface of the underlying politics of the plot. Of course not every movie is going to be a fundamental dissection of class, even I'm not that boring. I enjoyed it but found it too light and fleeting, too mundane. Maybe that was the point.

It's as if the movie was made to be referenced by youtube essayists 20 years later. Presentism, I know.

Superman (2025)

I had no intention of watching this film until my friends, and basically everyone on the internet, swore up and down that it was a pro-Palestine film. I still didn't believe them because how can such a massive franchise so deeply imbedded in Hollywood be anything like that. My friends fought me tooth and nail and finally made me watch it and I was pleasantly surprised. It's as clear as could be without being explicit. Sure, the invading country speaks Serbo-Croatian, but other than that it's clear cut that Boravia is the Israel stand in and Jarhanpur is the Palestine stand in. The Borovians have tanks, guns, US made weapons, and are "strong US allies", and the Jarhanpur has sticks and machete's to defend themselves with. Boravians are white, Jarhanpuris are brown. The film is not about Russia and Ukraine (obviously) because if it were, the US would be on the side of the smaller country being invaded. It's also not about the Bosnian genocide because it wouldn't have been two completely different looking people.

The movie was really good. As a Superman movie it was incredibly satisfying and showed that his whole deal is how do I stop the disaster without losing even a squirrel's life. Superman is naive, and powerful, and kind, and above all he has a clear moral compass. I have to admit I nearly cried.

We must remember that in real life there is no Superman. There are normal people, struggling, protesting, boycotting, fighting, dying, suing, sabotaging, striking, delaying, speaking, singing, crying, donating, and doing the real liberation working in a million other ways. It was really nice to see Green Lantern give the "Zionists" the middle finger, it was cathartic, but it was not real. Don't allow the film's happy ending lull you into passivity. In the real world we all have to be Superman.

Frontiers of Dreams and Fears

We often hear and read about Palestinian children after something horrible has been done to them by Israelis. It's far less common for us to see them living life as it is in Palestine and in the refugee camps in Lebanon. This film follows two 14 year old Palestinian girls as they live what is considered a normal life as a Palestinian refugee. They're so normal. I see my cousins in them. They do homework, have friends, talk about boys, eat, laugh, cry. Of course I knew this, but I hadn't seen anyone talk about it or show it. Always we see Palestinian children in the midst of Zionist made, US funded famine and genocide. The film follows as the girls travel to the barb wired border and meet other Palestinians, the scenes made me cry. All the while the Zionist presence threatens life. Near the end of the movie the Intifada begins and we see the reality. The cowardly blood lusting Israelis shooting at children throwing rocks. The movie is set in 2006. Two decades later, we still fight for Palestinian liberation.

Leila and the Wolves

Leila takes us back in time to the struggle against the British and the Zionists in Palestine and Lebanon. She shows the audience the role that women played in the struggle, a whole often diminished or ignored all together. The film does not deride the men who fought and died, in fact the film does not focus on them at all. They are acknowledged, mentioned, and recognized but they are not the focus. Instead we see how the women sacrificed all they had, risked life and limb to spy, smuggle weapons, misdirect enemy troops, and fight the enemy head on. There is no romanticism. It's ugly, and painful, and real.

The film is interspersed with film and photographs from the real events portrayed, and was filmed in dangerous places. Women have always been an integral part of the struggle, and we do ourselves a great disservice by not remembering that.

The Thing

The film begins with odd and ominous scenes of violence and horror, and at first neither you not the characters know what is going on. By the end of the film, the beginning scenes make total sense and you wish you could tell the characters. I see why this film is held in such high regard, Carpenter killed it.

1948: Creation and Catastrophe

Sometimes the best way to expose evil is to let it speak for itself. The documentary film makers interview old people who witnessed, perpetrated, or were victims of the Nakba. You might think that this isn't a both sides issue, that Israeli terrorists shouldn't be given any space to speak. I thought so too until I heard them speak. No remorse. Cold monsters that continue to be proud of their actions. They acknowledge the Nakba is genocide and they stand by it. Israelis are truly a vile people.

The Andromeda Strain

Phenomenal film making. The editing with the split screen and multiple views, the spacing and suspense, the whole plot. Heck, even the casting was great. I don't know any of the actors but I loved how normal their looked. No one was runway model attractive, no one had quips, it was all done so well.

Alien

Absolutely beautiful. The costumes, props, spaceship, all incredible! I had long known the whole plot of the film and all the twists due to internet cultural-osmosis and yet I enjoyed every moment watching this movie.

The Palestine Exception

Freedom of speech is a right in the United States, and like all rights and laws it is applied selectively and in ways that benefit those in power. We see this again in the Palestine Exception, free speech is great until students in the United States decide to use their voices to protest genocide, then they are violently shut down. The film shows the encampments and the reaction of academia to peaceful protest.

The Eid Gift

The Zionists steal your home, murder your family, ethnically cleanse the land, wage genocide on your people, and occasionally offer you the opportunity to visit home (you must pay them for the privilege of course and purchase their goods while you are there.) do you take it offer? It might be the only time you ever get to see your stolen home. You might be martyred by them before the next chance, before liberation. This impossible question is at the center of the documentary short film The Eid Gift. The answer may appear clear now but the reality is far more difficult.

Salt of this Sea

It's one thing to read about Palestine, talk to Palestinians, watch the news of Palestinian events, it's a whole other thing to experience Palestinian stories. The film may be fictional, but the checkpoints, the harassment, the abuse, the danger, the dispossession, the frustration and anger and pain. That is all real.

Salt of this Sea follows a Palestinian women as she returns to her homeland and finds it filled with a hostile and brutal occupier. Her mobility, language, appearance, love, and mere existence is an act of rebellion against the tyrannical apartheid state. You see plainly the Banality of Evil as everyone from TSA to illegal occupier participates in the oppression. Even those who seem outwardly progressive are shown to be simply acting the part in the shallowest sense.

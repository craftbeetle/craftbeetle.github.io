Nantou Case Study

Let us take the Chinese city of Nantou for example. Nantou is hot and humid most of the year, which is the climate I am most familiar with. Here we see two yearly average graphs that describe daily high and low temperatures and humidity with a very useful comfort rating derived from the dew point.

In a closed and air conditioned system, the goals are to maximize insulation, minimize air exchange. As the external environment becomes more uncomfortable the indoor environment remains the same but power consumption and thus cost increases.

While this affords us the ability to ignore the outdoor environment, we are now subject to some drawbacks: high operating costs and minimal fresh air.

The alternative method is to work with the external environment and minimize power usage. We can split our year into roughly 3 categories.

Hot (May - Sep) Medium (Mar - Apr & Oct - Nov) Cold (Dec - Feb)

Starting with the easiest category, cold. Where I live I have not had to use any central heating all through the winter.

House Keep south facing curtains open in the day and closed at night. Keep windows closed on the coldest days. I often had them open because I enjoyed the cold air. Double pane windows and insulated walls. Clothing Layers Long sleeves and pants Socks and a beanie Food Warm and spicy foods and drinks. Heating On the absolute coldest nights I used a small space heater in my bedroom. No use heating up unused spaced. Hot showers also help warm up after yard work in the cold and inject some humidity into the indoor air. Next is the medium transitional climate between cold and hot. In this phase the climate is not cold but also not swelteringly hot. I have not used air conditioning in this period. House Open windows to get maximum cross-flow. Keep curtains closed in the day to limit sun radiation heating the interior. Clothing Loose and breathable cotton. Wicking fabric. Food Cold drinks and frozen fruits. Hydrate Cooling Oscillating fans quickly dry sweat and cool the body down at very low power usage. Cold showers rapidly cool the body down, especially after outdoor work.

I have not yet experienced the hot period without air conditioning. I will update this section at the climate get hotter and more humid. I may find that there is a certain cut off point where not using air conditioning is no longer viable, or I may find ways to cope and be comfortable regardless.

Perspiration

(Most) humans are mammals. We sweat to cool down, which is actually a pretty rare trait in the animal kingdom. We also are relatively hairless as compared to horses, cows, and apes, some of the other animals that sweat. This combination of hairlessness and sweating allows us to cool very rapidly, especially in hot dry climates.

Real Feel

Real feel is a common term referring to Dew Point or Heat Index or Wet-Bulb. The human body is usually at about 97.7–99.5 °F. As mammals we produce our own heat (endothermogenisis) so our concern isn't so much finding the perfect temperature so much as it is being in conditions where our internal heat generation can produce enough heat to keep us warm enough, and our external heat dissipation can keep us cool enough. There is a wide range of temperatures and humidity where can manage, beyond that we either can't get rid of the heat or can't produce the heat fast enough to remain in thermostasis.

These are all methods of converting scientific data points to a metric that more adequately describe how it feels, or more accurately, how it impacts thermo-regulation.

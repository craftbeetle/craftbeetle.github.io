GAMES

Here live some of my thoughts on the games I have completed or played enough of. Sometimes I have a lot to say about the games I spend hours playing. Sometimes I play a game for a few hours, think it's neat, and put it down forever.

2025

Celeste

I had originally bounced off of Celeste the first few times I tried it. Something about the controls and getting stuck on some level or another. I picked it up again after finishing Silksong. The controls are much, much more simple and clumsy hot off the heels of Hornet's acrobatics. I found myself reaching for the abilities I had learned to rely on and instead found Madeline's simple jump and double jump. But, where I was disappointed with the lack of movement abilities, I was also hardened against difficulty by Silksong and found it much easier to just lock in and finish the game. I didn't 100% the game, though I'm sure I'll be hopping on once in a while to collect strawberries and cassettes.

ARC Raiders

Playing...

Hollow Knight: Silksong

What an experience! 3 months and 71 hrs later I have 100% completed Silksong. There's still a few things to do but those will come later, and a DLC has been announced already. I had an amazing time with the game as I did with Hollow Knight, and I'm glad I went through the majority of it, got two endings, and discovered most secrets before looking a single thing up. Silksong's movement and combat is significantly more fluid and agile than that of Hollow Knight and thus lends itself to a higher level of difficulty. You sprint and flip and bounce and dash, all in one fluid combo that extends through the whole game. The soundtrack perfectly accompanies the highs and lows of your adventure, and often times I find myself walking or standing still to take in the beauty of the world. It's no secret that I love bugs and in Silksong you meet countless amazing bugs with deep character and style. I could go on for a long time talking about Silksong and how good of a time I had with it. I spent a quarter of a year, nearly two work weeks with it. Every minute was a treat.

Sonic Mania

Sonic Mania is popularly regarded as a great Sonic game and masterful return to form developed by a small dedicated team. That all may very well be true, it's also true that I'm not a fan of Sonic. I think the character is great, I enjoyed the cartoon when I was young and the new films. The actual gameplay on the other hand I've never really enjoyed, neither in 2D nor in 3D. I decided to give this one a shot because I happen to have it on Epic (I have too many games on Epic and I don't really understand the business model. Is the idea to just throw so much Fortnite money at the PC gamer market that you force Epic into actually breaking in as a game store? Because the store and extra functionality itself is nowhere near Steam. Who knows, anyway...). As expected I didn't really have fun with it and dropped it after like 2 or 3 levels.

LEGO Builder's Journey

Played about half of the game, which isn't much considering it takes about 90 minutes to beat, and felt overwhelming boredom. Too simple, too basic, maybe it was made for small children. Also, single button controls seem clever until you start playing and realize that it only makes things annoying.

Overgrowth

I was first interested in Overgrowth because of the 2014 GDC talk the developer did explaining the player movement coding. I thought his system was so interesting. The game itself is a bit too technical for me honestly, while I found it really fun for many levels, it still feels like a high skill unforgiving competitive game with very little room for error. That's not inherently bad, many games pull it off quite well, but that combined with a very thin story and uninspired scenery makes they experience monotonous. After you kill a few different enemies in increasing numbers and armor and weapons you realize that that's all there is.

Mouthwashing

I didn't intend to play this game. I heard that a new indie horror game had everyone excited and casually ignored it. Then a good friend invited me to play it at his house on a big ol' CRT no less, so I did. There were 4 of us, each voicing a character and me voicing Anya and Curly. We had a great time, laughed our asses off. It surely was not the intended experience but I would not have played it any other way.

Sludge Life

Folks are rude and on edge, the place sucks, there's sludge everywhere. I ate some slugs, tags every surface I could reach, then dipped.

Dredge

It starts out simple enough, a fishing rod, an ominous fog, a hamlet with a dock. Then Dredge gradually releases the reins and soon you find yourself on the high seas angling for a specific fish to help someone and meddling with eldritch horrors beyond the imagination. I like it. It was peaceful, though a bit stressful at night in the beginning. I ended up catching a bunch of fish, visiting every island, helping some folk and completing the game.

Into The Breach

Mechs on a chess board, what more can you ask for? Well, maybe it's not the greatest game ever devised but it certainly was very fun. I unlocked some of the other mech teams and unlocked 3 out of 4 islands before I stopped playing. I enjoyed the tactical choices and there was definitely more to do and learn.

Darkest Dungeon

Sometimes a game just doesn't click. This one was such a case. I played it for a bit and didn't really enjoy it. Part of that was UI on the SteamDeck, but most of it was the game's mechanics themselves. Nothing egregious, just didn't interest me.

Antichamber

I enjoyed me time with Antichamber. The puzzles were well thought out, though perhaps smarter than I am. After 7 hours with the game I have filled out most of the image board but have not yet completed it or gotten all the colors. Either way I enjoyed my time with it.

Sulfur

A cute little game about shooting goblins in purgatory while hunting down a witch. It's got a cute art style and fun enough game play but it is a little too simple for repeated loops as a roguelike and it has a memory leak that causes it to turn into a slideshow by mid dungeon. To be fair this is all to be expected from an Early Access game, I do not fault it. I guess I'll just have to pick it up again when it fully releases and see if it has improved.

Super Meat Boy

Maybe I'm not as young and dexterous as I use to be. It's a repeating theme in these reviews. As I have reflected on the games I use to play and those I play now I have noticed a maturation. What I have lost in quick reflexes in and abundant free time, I have gained in appreciation of narrative and emotional depth.

Maybe it's all cope because I tried replaying some Super Meat Boy and couldn't get past the first boss which I had beat easily when I was younger.

Who's to know?

Night in the Woods

All I knew about this game was a single screenshot of Mae and Gregg on a bike and the vague idea of communism. What I found was a character driven story about the collapse of formerly industrial middle America and the very real personal struggles of people navigating that collapse. I enjoyed talking to each character every day, I enjoyed exploring Possum Springs and taking in the views, I enjoyed finding all the little hidden spaces that humanize a place. The choice to use anthropomorphic animals for the characters counterintuitively humanizes the characters because we cannot see their phenotype, we have to instead pay attention to their thoughts, feelings, actions. The ending, which I won't spoil, was as satisfying as the rest of the story. The guitar mini-game was fun and the music in the game was motivated and beautiful.

Gregg rulz ok.

Ape Out

Phenomenal sound design paired with a single visceral gameplay mechanic accompanied by very simple visuals that fit perfectly. It was a joy to play Ape Out. The game is clearly made with a lot of love and care and immense creativity.

Aperture Desk Job

Funny silly toilet desk game.

Metal Gear Rising: Revengence

I admit that before playing Metal Gear Rising: Revengence, I was not a fan of Kojima's. Maybe even a hater. Not in the true sense, I really loved the lore and world he made, but I could only ever enjoy it through lore videos and never could get into the games. I think that was partially because the first game of his I tried to play was MGS V which has a 2 hour intro that is mostly cutscenes and by the time I finished it I was already burned out and put the game down. Not to mention that when I first tried MGR I played it on Difficult, died once, and gave up. All that to say I came in with the wrong attitude and expectations. Years passed and I decided to give MGR another chance, if only to see Senator Armstrong. I was patient with the controls, respected the difficulty, sat back and enjoyed the cut scenes and low and behold it all clicked.

I love this game, it's silly, it's 2010s awesome, the music bangs, the action is over the top, the ideology isn't bad, and the pacing is perfect. When I played engaged with the game how it wanted to be engaged with I realized that the point of the cut scenes was to give you a bit of relief from the action. With this new perspective I am considering playing the other games in the series in release order to actually appreciate the overarching story being told. It really did help that I played it on my Steam Deck instead of my PC, being able to lay comfortably in bed makes the long cutscenes far more palatable.

PS: I love Jetstream Sam and Senator Armstrong. Blade Wolf is the goodest boy.

OlliOlli World

I got OlliOlli World because I enjoyed my time with OlliOlli2 - Welcome to Olliwood. World has a good character customization system and an actual plot and characters, but neither of those things is the point of the game and they have no real effect on it. I was disappointed with the actual gameplay. It's relatively shallow, repetitive, and mostly grinds. There is little feedback on actions taken and basically no need to do complex moves, other than the score. Famous 3D skating games succeed when they provide many challenges of different types: Speed, style, combo length, jumps, specific moves in order, etc...

I completed one of four worlds and did not feel compelled to continue playing.

2024

Need For Speed III: Hot Pursuit

I am a huge fan of 5th generation graphics. I didn't play video games back then so I don't have nostalgia for the style, I just think it looks really nice. In the same way that photographs didn't make the painting obsolete, realistic 3D graphics do not make crunchy low poly graphics obsolete. That being said I don't love everything about that era of games: the low FPS, the pop-in, and specifically the PS1's warble.

Retro 3D game preamble completed, Need for Speed III: Hot Pursuit is really fun! Looks amazing, sounds real good, hard to drive but once it clicks it clicks. At first I was bumping off the walls and crashing horribly in the hazards, but after some practice I was sticking to the line and drifting just the right amount. Highly enjoyable!

Bad North

It seems a little too simple at first. Just move your guys to kill their guys. The more of your guys the better. But as you go on from island to island the complexity and the fun begins to layer on. You start realizing that the skills, items, money, power ups, all have a place and a use. You start to realize there there are more optimal ways to fight.

By the time I felt I really got the hang of Bad North, I was quick switching units, shields first to absorb projectiles and charges then spears to do damage, I was putting spear-men at the top of stairs and slopes and at the mouth of choke-points to get an advantage, I was taking pot shots and retreating with my archers. The game is so active and dynamic, the stakes are high enough that you don't want to lose and low enough that you are confident experimenting and discovering new tactics.

I really enjoyed playing Bad North and will definitely be coming back to it once in a while.

VVVVVV

A short and sweet puzzle platformer that puts all of its effort into delivering a tight experience and it succeeds. Only a few hours long but no room is wasted. Similar to Celeste in a lot of ways.

Absolute Drift

A great idea but a very difficult execution. Driving as hard enough from a fixed perspective, drifting is ever harder. I was able to execute some pretty clean moves but it was inconsistent and not very satisfying. Maybe with more practice I could have gotten it but I just didn't feel compelled to go on.

Hades

I enjoyed my time with Hades. The art is beautiful, the writing and voice acting are natural, and the combat is fun. But, as is becoming a theme of my game reviews, I simply do not have the time to play games for as long as they want to be played. I would have loved to experience the story of Hades, but being a rogue like infinitely replayable game with incrementally drip-fed story, I could barely scratch the surface before I stopped playing.

Marvel Rivals

I enjoyed my time playing Marvel Rivals, though I am not one for Hero Shooters or competitive games. I simply don't have the time to get good at them. Great game though, best I've played of the genre.

20 Minutes Till Dawn

Let me start off by saying that I did in fact play this game for more than 20 minutes, but I did not beat it. It's a Vampire Survivor like with pretty good gameplay but not anything I expect to be replaying any time soon. It's good at what it set out to do but seeing as I have not played Vampire Survivor, I've got no idea if it's as good at what seems like the gold standard.

BPM: Bullets Per Minute

BPM is really odd in that I understood it to be a rhythm game like Hi-Fi Rush so I started out trying to get all my actions to line up to the rhythm and I struggled. Then I gave up on that and just played it as a regular FPS, shooting and jumping and dashing whenever I want and I did much better. I had a fun time with it, but with it's main gimmick so weak I'm not hooked.

BattleBit Remastered

Impressive how much a three man team can achieve when concerned only with good gameplay and not worrying about the best graphics or the most aggressive monetization or advertising. It's especially poignant as BattleBit Remastered released around the same time the newest Battlefield game flopped. This, and the success of many other indie games, as well as the slew of recent AAA flops and layoffs is a clear sign of the readjustment of the industry. Interest free money is gone, the period of massive growth is over, labor is up, and the video game corporations are being out competed by solo devs and indie studios.

That all being said, and as much fun as I had with this game, at time of writing it seems to have been abandoned by the developers which is actually a bit of a rare case when it comes to success indie games. AAA corporation on the other hand are more than happy to shutter whole studios making widely popular games simply because profits weren't high enough.

Chivalry 2 was much like Chivalry 1, a great time. I played it for a while longer than I did the first and found it to just be an overall improved experience on all fronts. Sekiro: Shadows Die Twice

FromSoft nails it again with an amazing game. It was difficult from start to finish and all the better for it. Every time I struggled against a boss and finally figured them out and beat them felt amazing, and returning to old areas only to find that I was easily beating old foes with little effort was very rewarding. I must admit that I stopped playing the game right at the final boss. Maybe someday I will come back to it and truly complete it. I think my mistake was taking too long of a break after getting stuck and losing my muscle memory. Regardless of not completing the game, I still enjoyed every moment, even the repeated deaths. I vividly remember every zone and boss, which is a testament to the quality of the craftsmanship.

Monument Valley

A cute little game kill a few hours. None of the puzzles were stumpers but the vibes were clean and it didn't overstay its welcome. I also played the bonus levels and found them to be about the same. I first learned about this game from House of Cards. That did not age well, but the game was good enough to survive that hit.

Downwell

I played a bit of Downwell on Play Pass. I found it to be okay, nothing compelling. Obviously it's only meant to be a little mini game but it didn't have the draw found in Flappy Bird or 2048.

Slay The Spire

I played this game in short bursts on my phone over the course of a week. I think that is really what it was designed for, I can't image sitting down at a computer and focusing on it. The mechanics have enough depth to be engrossing until the end of the game. I slew the spire and unlocked all 4 characters, though The Silent was my favorite and was the one I used to beat the game. Overall a very enjoyable experience.

Sifu

I had a passing interest in the game and finally decided to pick it up after Jacob Geller made a video about it. It has such an amazing progression, from the episodic aging to the true ending. I started out as I assume most do, running around getting hit, dying, barely making it through levels. But as I got better at the game I slowly mastered the flow of bait and punish, as well as the rush and strike. I easily made it through large portions of the game untouched and finally finished the game first in the bad ending and then the good. The bonus stages were also a blast. In a strange turn of events, this game caused me to want to play Sekiro. I needed more of that beautiful parrying gameplay.
Side note: I played on Linux and experienced a GPU memory leak. The game would get progressively slower until it became a slide show and I had to reboot it. I finally found the cause in one of the graphics settings and it was fixed.

Cyberpunk 2077

I have to admit I was a huge hater when this game came out. I had absolutely no hype for it, didn't watch any trailers and new very little about it. So to me, all I saw was a game struggling and floundering, and developer promises broken and a poor product sold at full price. I wrote the game off and moved on. Fast forward to the 2.1 patch. On a whim I broke my promise of never playing this game and bought it and the DLC on sale. Little did I know the game would become one of my favorites of all time. I think it really helped that I didn't have any expectation for the game, I know a lot of people expected an RPG with dialogue and decisions and character progression, and of course were disappointed with a mostly linear game. Meanwhile I came in expecting to shoot a bunch of goons for about 10 hours and was happily surprised with 100 hrs of beautiful realistic and well acted characters, a compelling story, and very fun gameplay. Johnny Silverhand lives rent free in my head.

Baldur's Gate III

I got BG3 months after its first release and after much praise from my friends. It wasn't on my radar and I didn't plan on getting it, and because I never watch any ads or trailers, I really didn't even know what it was other than it's association with the first two Baldur's Gate games and the DnD world. I was pleasantly surprised and engrossed when I finally started playing it. I made a half-elf rogue, quickly gravitated to Shadowheart (respeced as paladin), Karlach (respeced as fighter), and Wyll (kept as warlock). I also played plenty with Lae'zel and Astarion. Armored Core VI: Fires of Rubicon

This was the first Armored Core game I played. I don't do hype as a rule and I avoid all trailers and advertising so this game did not pop into my radar until release day. On a whim and knowing little about Armored Core except for some videos about the first few I decided to buy it. I'm glad I did because for the next few weeks I played it to completion and had a fine time doing so. I love how mechanical and heavy it is, while maintaining an incredible level of fluidity and control. I've always loved mechs, but compared to Pacific Rim and Gundam, this style is by far my favorite. Not so heavy as to be an oversized Rockem Sockem robot, not so agile as to be space acrobatics. Very mechanical and real. I do want to check out the Battletech video games to see how they feel in comparison. This game was in fact the inspiration for me buying my Battletech minis.

Splitgate

My friends and I played Splitgate regularly for a few weeks at its peek and we had a blast. We played at the perfect time to enjoy great lobbies of mixed skill and new techniques being discovered all the time. If you figured something out you would dominate multiple games until people caught up with you which really encourage creativity. At first we played it like a shooter, moving normally and going for regular kills. After a while though everyone adapted and it was no longer enough. We then learned all the movement tech, the portal physics, and great team communication to get a much needed edge on our opponents. It's a shame the game when out of style, replaced by the much inferior and now dead Halo Infinite.

Going Under

Corporate work sure is a slog. Aggro Crab's going under is a cute delve into an assortment of modern corporate jobs and their pitfalls as formulated under capitalism. The goal of each "dungeon" is to be the boss (my favorite of which is the Caffiend). The weapon mechanic keep the game feeling fresh and the writing is very human. The game is limited in it's economic analysis by not pushing for unionization, which would actually help protect the Joblins and other workers. Instead the game essentially stops at criticizing startups, the gig economy, modern dating, and crypto. All valid criticisms but somewhat hollow without the underlying cause of capitalism being analyzed. Regardless I loved the game, completed it and played a bit of the infinite dungeon before happily retiring it.

Totally Accurate Battle Simulator

Banished

The Last of Us

First let's address the fascist shaped elephant in the room: The Last of Us Part 1 and 2, and the show, were written by a genocidal Zionist who has actively called for the murdering of all Palestinians. In response to seeing a video of Palestinians killing Israeli soldiers (The right of colonized people's right to violent resistance against the occupying force is protected in two different place in international law: UNGA Resolution 3314 (1974) and UNGA Resolution 37/43 (1982)) in Ramallah (which is even by loose standard of the UN considered Palestinian land) Neil Druckmann said "Oh, man, if I could just push a button and kill all these people (Palestinians) that committed this horrible act, I would make them feel the same pain that they inflicted on these people (Zionist soldiers)".

The politics of the games are not spared of course, Druckmann confirms that they are an allegory for the Zionist occupation of Palestine, and we know where he stands on that. So what can I now say about the game? Can I truly sit here and tell you that at the time I played it, and for years afterwards I thought it was amazing and that it easily was one of my favorite games of all time? Would that wash the genocide flavored taste out of your mouth? I can't separate art from artist, can you? Art is inherently political, it must be. Even when it tries to be apolitical it defacto supports the status quo by failing to challenge it. The Last of Us isn't apolitical, it's the product of so much sadistic torture and deliberate torture for the sake of the empire.

Ghost of Tsushima

Ghost of Tsushima is easily one of the best games I have ever played. I first played it on normal but after about an hour I turned the difficulty to max and it clicked. The whole point of samurai and ninja is the long periods of calm punctuated by explosions of highly lethal violence. Turning the difficulty up made it so that if I didn't make every strike and basically not get hit I would die, which compliments this game's aesthetic and theme as well as its mechanics.

Both stealth and action are viable so I hunted in hiding until I was discovered when I went loud. I was so immersed that I would clean my sword, bow, and play a song after every fight because it just felt right to pay respect, especially after beating important characters. There was nothing forcing me to do that, nor was there any reward, but it was what Jin would have done.

Playing as Jin Sakai, exploring the beautiful island of Tsushima, fighting against the mongols and traitor samurai, living in that world, it was all such a great experience.

Either way I dropped off the game. I'm glad I played it when I did.

Dead Cells

I've played Dead Cells on and off since release. I got one boss cell once and ever since then I haven't been able to beat it and get more.

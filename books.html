BOOKS

Reading is a fundamental pillar of my life and the way I was raised. I find that while other forms of media have great value and strengths, books remain the best way to delve deeply into a subject. A book's demand for exclusive cognitive attention makes gives it the ability to convey more than any other medium. My goal is to read 50 books a year, which is about a book a week. Here are my thoughts on the books I have read.

The Message

Ta-Nehisi Coates was introduced to me through an interview he did with Novara Media where he talked about The Message. I did not read it then, but I did think his observations of apartheid were clear, though I did not need him to tell me that.

Later on a good friend recommended the book so I listened to the audiobook narrated by Coates himself. There, through his evocative narration I learned about Coates' life, his journey, and his reason for writing The Message. That The Message is a book long correction of an error he has made in invoking Israel a potential model for the reparation to Africans in America as he outlines in The Case For Reparations. In doing so I had learned about his wrong doing through his correction, and I grew to respect him even more for that honestly.

Certainly a worthwhile read or listen. Ta-Nehisi aims to haunt the reader and listener and succeeds.

Returning to Haifa

Heart wrenching. I felt myself fill with anger, and hate, then sadness, and mourning, then determination as I journeyed through this short story. I felt what Said felt, what Safiyya felt, and what Faris felt. Just as I was most strongly needing Said to take his house back, Kanafani gives Faris' story to temper that feeling. Just as I most strongly mourned the past, Said realizes the homeland is the future.

Blackshirts and Reds

Parenti lays out a balanced defense of the soviet union in specific and communism in general in Blackshirts and Red, and explains how communism's opponents tear it down and destroy it. Highly informative and ultimately tragic as vast swaths of the global population achieved then lost communism for various reasons from within and without. Parenti is of course a must read for anyone interested in communism and socialism, and for those interested in how capitalists and fascists ruin life for everyone. At the very least, everyone should listen to the Yellow Parenti lecture.

The Repairer of Reputations

An old classic in horror, written in the 1890s and commonly found in The King in Yellow. I really enjoyed this short story. It was incredibly immersive and the images it painted were incredible. Right at the end of the story I realized that I had believed the narrator and only in the last moments did I think back and realize there was no proof for anything they had claimed. Amazing!

Hellstar Remina

I don't like horror. All my friends know it, I avoid it as much as possible. Not because it scares me but because it does't interest me.

That being said, I am now engaging with it for the purpose of learning it to be able to run a game of Mothership. Hellstar Remina is a work by the renowned horror mangaka, Junji Ito. I heard of him and praise of his work primarily in association with The Enigma of Amigara Fault. Hellstar Remina is beautifully drawn and well written. I can't say that I was moved by it in any way, but it was certainly not a dull read. I found it more funny than scary.

L'Hégémonie Culturelle

This book was lent to me by a very good friend almost as a joke because he gave me the french translation. The book was originally written in italian so there was no reason to read a french translation instead of an english one except for the fact that I could, so I did. The reading was easy but the understanding not so much. I use to be fluent in french but I haven't used it for over a decade, so reading was more a de-rusting than a theoretical learning experience for me. I'll have to read it again in english to really understand it but already I am more comfortable speaking and thinking in french.

Either way I had a really fun time with it.

The Old Man and the Sea

Another old man trying to catch yet another fish. I won't spoil how it goes but the journey is so well crafted. There are no chapters, just one long tale and it is so much better for it. This is exactly the kind of story I want read to me in a quiet but emotive voice on a sunset beach. Oddly specific, but read the story and you may have a similar experience of peace and humanity.

The Revolution of 1936-1939 in Palestine

Ghassan Kanafani widely known in the west for fiction writing, but his non-fiction political writing is far more well known around the world. The Revolution of 1936-1939 in Palestine is an analysis of the material conditions that lead up to the revolution and a dissection of the causes for the revolution's defeat by the British and Zionist forces. The Revolution's crushing is correctly identified as the precursor to the 1948 Nakba and the events that follow. Kanafani's writing is concise and to the point, he cites all of his sources and evidence for his analysis and lays out a clear view of the events and their impact. During my reading I kept thinking of the parallels in our current time and place and how we can avoid the pitfalls of the past.

The Left Hand of Darkness

Another of Ursula K. Le Guin's Hanish Cycle novels, The Left Hand of Darkness has many of the same themes as The Disposessed while on a different planet with unique circumstances. I liked the story and the ideas it explores. The Dispossessed is still my more favorite of the two.

The Green Book

No wonder Obama had him assassinated, Gaddafi had some really good ideas. Did I agree with every single idea and statement in the book? Maybe not. But his overall political, economic, and social theory are sound.

The Name of the Wind

First of all, the magic system in this world is really cool. It's what you would get if you could quantum entangle whole objects or processes instead of particles. The magic feels and is treated like a science. I enjoyed Kvothe's escapades with the troupe, in the city, and in the university. Though he is a boy wonder he still doesn't solve all of his problems easily, far from it. The one bit I did not enjoy, and it was an overly long bit being as it was most of the last third of the book was the romance. I just was not interested in all of that, didn't find it compelling. It's not that it comes out of left field either, the book telegraphs this all through out, from the songs of mythical ancient lovers to the characters themselves saying it straight up in the very beginning of the book. What I did not expect was how much time was spent focusing on the details of teenage romance. The book is still great. I don't think I'll be reading the second in the series though because it seems like the third and final book will not be coming out any time soon and I just don't need another cliffhanger in my life.

The First Time Manager

This book is surprisingly good. It was another assigned reading for my job. I found it very clear and informative on the practical skills of management. Of course managerialism is never the goal, but we do exist in a capitalist society as of now and so we might as well understand how to navigate it. It is structured as a manual and so whenever there is a need, one can easily flip to the relevant section and read the author's advice. That advice is generally what you would get from a more senior competent manager. Worth reading if you are a manager or work with them, will give you insight into the machine.

The Servant

The Servant is a book about leadership which attempt to change old corporate habits from old school management to a more progressive leadership. It was assigned reading and it wasn't horrible. Well written for what it is and generally inoffensive, except for the anti-union bits. The target audience are middle to upper-middle class managerial liberals, so obviously a material analysis of work place conditions, or the idea of self governed democratic labor was out of the question. Instead we get paternal pseudo-religious platitudes.

A Dying Colonialism

As I read about the Algerian fight for liberation I gained a deeper understanding of French Islamophobia. In the course of their colonialism they had a concerted effort to remove the Algerian women's hijab as a way of westernizing them and asserting cultural dominance. What they instead got was those women resisting even harder and using the hijab as a weapon against oppression, by either hiding their identity as they conducted resistance actions or by removing their hijab thus tricking the french into believing that had won her over and lowering their guard against her. Now the french continue to ban the hijab as a vindictive act against their former colonial subjects.

The french attempted to destroy the Algerian understanding of family and culture yet found that the Algerian people, after a moment of disoriented despair and withdrawal, returned with a righteous might. Once the Algerian people decided they wanted to be free, there was no stopping them, and rightfully so. Even a cursory search of French atrocities in Algeria is enough to make anyone with a consistence resist them. For example, between 8 May – 26 June 1945 the french massacred up to 45,000 Algerians in the cities of Sétif and Guelma.

As with all colonial resistance, the oppressors are fortunate that the oppressed do not seek revenge, simply freedom and justice. All those who wanted to join the liberation struggle, be they Muslim Algerian, Jewish Algerian, or Western Algerian were allowed to fully participate and were welcome to remain in the newly liberated Algeria as equals. This is the faith of all colonial endeavors, no matter how advanced they may seem. I am now interested in learning about the Moroccan revolution against the French and the Spanish.

Ishmael

My whole experience with Ishmael was wonderful. It started off with a conversation with great friend about this, that or the other. She then told me she brought something for me and produced an old copy of Ishmael, bought from a thrift store some time ago, her bookmark still in it. I was so touched by the gift that I hungry read the book in a few days. The paper was yellowed with time, the cellulose broken down into a sweet sent.

Ishmael teaches the main character, and in doing so teaches the reader, that the world is not made for humans, but that humans were made for it. That when people see the world as theirs, they inherently seek to conquer and invariably destroy it. And while I disagree on the theological points, I do agree that humanity should seek to better live in the world, alongside all of creation, than to dominate and annihilate it.

In the spirit of being gifted the book, I then passed it along to my parents. And after they finish it I'll give it to a friend, so that all may read what Ishmael has to teach.

You can't escape the cage if you can't find the bars.

Value, Price and Profit

Marx makes the relationship of value, price, and profit very clear. We often still hear about how increasing wages would increase the price of goods and services but in reality that is not the case. It simply reduces profits for the bourgeoisie, profits they derive from the unpaid portion of the proletariat's labor.

Wage-Labour and Capital

I was already familiar with Real Wages, but I had not thought of Relative Wages which are really the root of the income inequality by definition. As well I have a new appreciation for the gig economy as as the ideal capitalist labor market, where all tasks are fractalized and staccato, disconnecting the laborer from the labor and for each other, opening the door for infinite scabs.

Abandoned Palestine Refugee

I met Ihmayed Ali and his daughter at a Palestinian book talk where they shared about their lives and experiences as Palestinian refugees. The talk was moving and eye opening. I then bought and read his book and was blown away by the images he shared. Ihmayed's autobiography is written in a very vivid and honest way. The things he goes through, the oppression and abuse, the survival and success. His story is representative of large portions of the Palestinian population, a people who endure despite incredible hardship. I highly recommend this book to anyone seeking a personal look at the Palestinian story.

Moby Dick

This book is absolutely massive. Melville clearly loves and knows much about whaling and he wants to share it all and it takes 600 pages to do so. I didn't love the book in all honesty but I do respect it. It is one of the oldest books I've read and had it come out in recent decades someone would have made him cut it down but I'm glad it exists in its entirety. A book about whales ought to be massive. I wouldn't recommend most people read it but the audiobook by William Hootkins is very good. I switch to it about half way through reading my physical copy.

Principles of Communism

An easy introduction into communist theory. This is absolutely a must read that lays out communism for the layman. I found it put already known and understood concepts into a new perspective, shed a light on some connection previously missed.

Strong Towns

In Strong Towns, Charles explains the way in which cities use to be built gradually and how that has changed into an unsustainable model of growth. He makes points about how single family homes are subsidized by downtown businesses, and about the debt cities find themselves with when they try to make themselves attractive to developments. What Charles fails to do is question the underlying economic system, and is instead content on working within capitalism. This is not surprising and he identifies as a conservative.

To Kill A Mockingbird

If ever there was a manual on how to be a good father, this would be a contender. This also goes for what it means to be a man. The way Harper Lee describes Atticus's interactions with his children and his community and the high regard in which they hold him shows what a man of integrity is, and shoots dead the idea that people in the past were simply a product of their time. A wonderful read and a pretty good movie.

The Dispossessed

A beautiful book that explores one possible way that anarcho-communism could exist. Le Guin does a great job of constructing a believable society with all of its struggles and beauty. Even if this isn't the exact way I imagine an ideal world, I find inspiration and hope in the refutation of the status quo as all that there ever could be. If this is a plausible formulation of society, what else is possible? A worthwhile and captivating read.

Children of Memory

So far we've had spiders, octopus, true aliens (insert "Nano Machines, Son!" audio sting), as well an increasingly moody human AI, and neo-humans. What's left? Why it's psychically linked corvids and a twist so good I won't even explain. Unlike the other two books, this one is a real mental trip. Elder race is linear but jumps perspectives. The Children of Time has a spider past and human present timeline it jumps between. And the Children of Ruin has two pasts, one on each planet, and a present. This book has a looping, recursive, bleeding timeline. As if you wrote the events of the story on a stack of paper and it got wet, words from one time transferring to another. It won't make total sense until it all makes sense in the end. Definitely worth a read.

2001: A Space Odyssey

Written with the intent of being adapted into a film, 2001 A Space Odyssey is mostly vibes and aesthetics. I had vivid images of the stations, space ships, and the cosmos. I hadn't watched the film until after I read the book so I found the book quite enjoyable.

Children of Ruin

So jumping spiders, ancient human AI, and neo-humans weren't enough? Now you want octopus and truly alien life? Okay! The second book in the Children of Series explores another set of worlds with new life forms and the way they developed. I am personally very fond of the octopus, I identify with and strive to argue like they do. They give it their all, use their logic and emotion, then, if they are truly convinced, they go all in and spread the message as if it were their own. The trick to this method is balance, you have to be sufficiently hard headed that only the best ideas with the best evidence can convince you, but you also have to be sufficiently sincere that once convinced you become a beacon of that idea. If the octopus's motive force is expression, then the other alien's motive is experience. The other alien seeks novelty at all costs, but in doing so is apt to destroy what it loves. There is much to learn from both creatures, and of forgiveness. Another banger from Tchaikovsky.

Born

A neat view into the origins of the Punisher. One can easily see how plausible this course of character development is and how it leads to one to believe that they are the law and that their violence is justified. A valuable window into cop culture.

Head Lopper

Head Lopper achieved exactly what it set out to do. It was beautiful and entertaining. At no point do you feel the head lopper could fail, and that's okay. I enjoyed reading the printed trade in one quiet summer night. I may read the other entries in the series in the future.

Children of Time

I don't think it's possible to read this book and not fall in love with jumping spiders, and don't get me started on Dr. Avarna Kern. Tchaikovsky has a gift for perspective. He has the ability to turn his mind into a blank slate, throw away our human preconceptions, and start a new from an alien perspective. How would a spiders build a society? How would they react if we suddenly wanted to take their planet? How would one of humanity's brightest minds deal with isolation and a fundamental change in cognitive ability? All beautifully explored question in this book and much more.

1984

What can I say about 1984 that hadn't been said already? I originally decided to read this book so that I could confidently say "I read 1984". I only half joke. But as I got into the book I discovered what all the hype was about. It wasn't mind blowing but I also read it in a post 1984 world which has for nearly 80 years been deeply affected by it. My most appreciated part of the book is how long ago it was written and how it remains relevant today.

Solaris

Solaris was originally written in Russian and the translation is of disputed quality. This matters particularly because this book, while set on an alien planet, occurs mainly in the thoughts of the main character. I enjoyed the explorations of sentience and cognition, and the soft dreamlike horror that builds up.

Elder Race

I was in the mall book store with a friend just killing time when the cover of this book caught my eye. I knew nothing of Tchaikovsky at the time so I had no idea that this was a soft sequel to his Children of Series. I read the first few pages in that book store and immediately loved it. I bought it on mid day Saturday, and by Sunday I had finished it. That's not a testament to my reading speed so much as it is of the books quality. The dual language concept and the science as magic concept were so good that I needed to know what else he had written, and a few days later the Children of Series arrived at my doorstep.

Transmetropolitan

This comic, and Spider Jerusalem, are one of my favorites. Real politics and journalism in a near future US city. It gave me the same feeling of appreciation for journalism that A Taxi Driver (the Korean film) gave me. The world needs more Spider Jerusalem's that do true honest journalism at all costs and by all means necessary.

Snow Crash

Right as the Cyberpunk 2077 conversation was warming up, and after watching Action Button's 8 hour review (yes, in one sitting), I was still unwilling to give the game a chance. But, what I getting really interested in was science fiction and cyberpunk. I decided to pick this book up and I was not disappointed. One of the seminal works in the cyberpunk genre, and the origin of the term "metaverse", it paints a funny version of the cyberpunk dystopia we've come to expect. Yes, the killing, poverty, and misery are all there as substrate for the tech megacorps to exploit. But the characters and narration lift the mood and focuses on the fun. Easily recommended.

Brave New World

I was recommended this book by a friend. They described it as "The Other 1984". I see the similarities but the themes not the same. The most striking aspect of the book is how normal creating people for specific roles is, and how the few intellectual elites are still bound up in the odd society they are a part of.

Paper Girls

Hands down one of my favorite graphic novels. I love every character and their development. The time travel is done really well and, like many great stories, all the fun action and science fiction ultimately serve a very personal coming of age story. The Amazon show was really good too but it was canceled after the first season. We cannot have nice things.

Dune

Dune is science fiction at its finest: an exploration of real human life, relationships, politics, material conditions, religion, etc all through a medium fictionalized enough to drop your guard. That's what makes the best science fiction, the abstracted layer that allows us to explore reality in play. You may not want to talk about the invasion of the Iraq but you do want to talk about Arrakis. Imperialism and colonialism for the sake of resources such as oil, gas, and mineral? No. Spice and water? Yes. There is a component of the white savior, inspired by Lawrence of Arabia, and there is an analog Sufi Islam rather than the approximately 90% Sunni reality, but it works. My favorite part of reading Dune was the use of Arabic as the "mystical" language instead of the usual Latin or a fictional language. As an Arabic speaker I had a slightly different reading experience than the non-Arabic speaker in that I got hints far in advance of where the plot was going. All in all Dune is an incredible book. I read it a few years before the first and second movies and I am glad I did. Highly recommended.

Pathfinder

I remember Pathfinder as being a great science fiction novel that had a fun twist with space-time travel and time loops. My dad also liked and ended reading the whole series which is something I really should do, that and read the Ender's game series in full.

Ender's Game

I read Ender's Game a long time ago in elementary school and I still remember it well. Card paints a fascinating picture of a fascist future fighting aliens and the consequences thereof. I won't spoil the ending but I will say that this book really clicked with a really young me.

The Law of Ueki

The Law of Ueki was one of the first manga I ever read and I read it before watching any anime so it essentially set a tone, a standard in my mind. Little did I know it was subverting many of the tropes of anime and manga by making the main characters whole ambition to simply protect the environment and be good to the people around him. Ueki couldn't care less about beating others, getting power, or even looking cool. All Ueki wants is for trees to grow, the air to be clean, and the people to be safe and happy. And that resonated with me. He's not shy to fight when necessary but he doesn't relish in it, doesn't make it his primary ambition. Ueki loves life.

The Legend of Zelda - Major's Mask Manga

This is a neat adaptation of Majora's Mask into a manga. I think adapting relatively linear video games may be the easiest type of adaptation to pull off and pull it off they did. While I haven't actually played Majora's Mask I have watched my fair share of walkthroughs, analysis, and speedruns. The manga was pretty darn good.
